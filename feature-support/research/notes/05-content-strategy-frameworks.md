# SRC-020: Content Strategy Frameworks — Hub/Spoke, Hero/Hub/Hygiene, Content Pillars

**Source type:** Content marketing methodology
**Credibility:** Moderate-high (industry frameworks with wide adoption, multiple independent sources)
**Date accessed:** 2026-03-26
**Sources:** Equinet Academy Hero-Hub-Hygiene guide (equinetacademy.com), LZC Marketing hub-and-spoke guide (lzcmarketing.com), Contently content pillars (contently.com), Digital Marketing Institute content pillars (digitalmarketinginstitute.com), Search Engine Journal hub-spoke model (searchenginejournal.com), Terra hub-and-spoke guide (terrahq.com), Powerhouse Studios hero-hub-hygiene (wearepowerhousestudios.com)

## Key Findings

### Three Major Content Strategy Frameworks

**1. Hero / Hub / Hygiene (Google's 3H Model)**
Originally developed by Google for YouTube strategy, now widely applied across all content:
- **Hero** (10%): Big, emotional, high-production content. Quarterly or annual. Purpose: reach new audiences.
- **Hub** (30%): Regular, episodic, themed content. Weekly/monthly. Purpose: nurture existing audience.
- **Hygiene/Help** (60%): Evergreen, SEO-driven, problem-solving content. Always on. Purpose: capture search intent.

Recommended ratio: 10% Hero, 30% Hub, 60% Hygiene (multiple sources cite this).

**2. Hub and Spoke (Content Architecture)**
A structural model for organizing content:
- **Hub**: Central long-form pillar page on a broad topic (1,500-2,000+ words)
- **Spokes**: Detailed articles on sub-topics that link back to the hub
- Benefits: SEO authority building, internal linking structure, user navigation

Recommended starting point: 3-5 pillar hubs, each with 5-10+ spoke articles.

**3. Content Pillars (Topic-Based)**
- 3-5 core themes that align with brand positioning and audience needs
- All content maps to one or more pillars
- Ensures topical authority and prevents content drift

Example (Contently): Strategy, Digital Transformation, Storytelling, ROI, Trending.

### Key Distinction: Strategy vs. Architecture vs. Cadence

These frameworks address different problems:
- **Content Pillars** = what you talk about (topics)
- **Hub/Spoke** = how content relates to each other (architecture)
- **Hero/Hub/Hygiene** = how often and what type (cadence + format)

A complete content strategy uses all three: pillars define territory, hub/spoke structures organize it, and 3H determines production rhythm.

### Relevance to Brand Compass

Brand Compass Phase 3 produces **Content Territories** (topics owned, contributed to, avoided) — this maps roughly to Content Pillars but without the operational framework.

Phase 8 Practical Toolkit includes "Content Territories" as a section, but it's a strategic classification, not an actionable content plan.

What's missing:
1. **Content pillar definitions** with audience mapping — which pillar serves which audience segment?
2. **Hub/spoke architecture** — how pillars translate to content structure
3. **Cadence recommendations** — Hero/Hub/Hygiene ratios adapted to client's capacity
4. **Channel-voice matrix** — how voice modulates across platforms (already identified as gap in Phase 4 SRC-015)

### How Content Pillars Differ from Content Territories

Brand Compass's Content Territories (Phase 3/8) classify:
- Topics the brand **owns** (authority/expertise)
- Topics the brand **contributes to** (perspective but not authority)
- Topics the brand **avoids** (off-brand or risky)

Content Pillars add:
- **Audience intent mapping** — which pillar captures which search/social intent
- **Content type mapping** — which format best serves each pillar
- **Frequency/volume guidance** — how much content per pillar
- **Performance metrics** — what success looks like per pillar

The territories are the strategic input; the pillars are the operational output.
