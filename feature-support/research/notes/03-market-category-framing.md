# Source Note: Market Category Framing in Brand Strategy

**Source ID:** SRC-011
**Type:** Multi-source synthesis (agency practices, category design literature)
**Processed:** 2026-03-26
**Credibility:** Moderate-high — triangulated across multiple practitioner sources and strategy frameworks
**Sources consulted:** Duffy Agency (category checklist), Atin Studio (category design), eau³ (category practitioners), Elements Brand Management, Prophet/Aaker (category strategy), Ohh My Brand (personal brand category), April Dunford (positioning/category relationship), Play Bigger framework (background)

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Category as Distinct Step vs. Folded Into Positioning

**Split in practice:**
- **Standard brand agencies:** Category is discovered during competitive analysis and positioning, not designed. Answer to "what bucket does your audience put you in?"
- **Category design practitioners:** Category is actively engineered. Naming the category is itself a strategic deliverable.

The agency management literature: "The category makes the brand, not the other way around."

April Dunford's framing: market category selection is the last step of positioning, not the first — you need differentiated value and best-fit customer before judging which category best points people toward that value.

### 2. Standard Agency Category Process

1. **Form a category hypothesis** — What bucket does the client believe they're in?
2. **Validate against audience shopping behavior** — Key principle: use the audience's categories, not the client's internal language
3. **Map competitors** — Who else is in that bucket? What are baseline expectations?
4. **Decide: compete within or create adjacent** — Position within existing category or define a new one
5. **Name and claim** — If creating new, name it explicitly

Duffy Agency: "It is important to talk with [prospects] and use their categories and not your own."

### 3. Positioning Within vs. Category Creation

**Positioning within:**
- Assumes category exists and is recognized
- Strategy: "We are the [adjective] one" — cheaper, simpler, enterprise-grade
- Less market education needed; faster sales cycles
- Goal: exploit leader's weaknesses

**Category creation:**
- Argues current category is broken, insufficient, or doesn't exist
- Strategy: Name a new problem, then name the solution
- Examples: HubSpot → "Inbound Marketing" (not "blogging tools"), Gong → "Revenue Intelligence" (not "call recording"), Drift → "Conversational Marketing" (not "chatbots")
- Atin Studio: "If you cannot name the container, you cannot own it"
- Requires significant market conditioning; longer sales cycles

### 4. Category Work for Individuals vs. Organizations

**For organizations:** Market education, analyst relations, sales cycles, investor framing. Revenue-generation play.

**For solo practitioners/thought leaders:**
- **Goal is name-topic synonymy** — "your name becomes almost interchangeable with that topic" (Ohh My Brand)
- **Proprietary frameworks replace product naming** — A named, structured way of thinking creates a "mini-category" where the person is automatically the pioneer
- **Contrarian POV is the category signal** — "Most experts focus on X; I focus on Y because Z" effectively names a micro-category
- **Without category framing, default to job title** — "a [job title] who does [thing]" — competing on resume rather than perspective
- Examples: Sinek's "Start With Why," Brown's "vulnerability research" are category names in practice

### 5. What Happens When Category Isn't Addressed

**Consistent finding: brands default to whatever category the audience imposes — usually the most generic available.**

- Duffy Agency: Without deliberate choice, you inherit hygiene factors of whatever generic bucket you land in
- April Dunford (via Atin Studio): "If you don't give them a name, they will give you one — and it will likely be 'It's like X, but smaller.'"
- Prophet: Without defined category, brand building has no anchor — activity diffuses rather than compounds
- For thought leaders: no category claim means competing on credentials and volume rather than perspective

## Relevance to Brand Compass

Brand Compass currently handles category indirectly across phases:
- **Phase 3 "territory":** what word/phrase the client wants to own
- **Phase 3 "contrarian POV":** implicitly, what existing category framing they're rejecting
- **Phase 3 "Unifying Lens":** closest analog for solo practitioners — asks whether the person is defining a new category across their topic range — but framed as optional

**The gap:** No explicit prompt for "what category does your audience currently put you in, and is that the right one?" Territory and contrarian POV approach category from inside-out (what do you want to be known for?). Category design also requires outside-in (what does your audience already think you are? Does that need disrupting or refining?).

**Finding tag:** COMPLICATED — Brand Compass captures territory and contrarian POV, which are category-adjacent. But it lacks the outside-in category question and doesn't explicitly present "create a new category" as a strategic option. The Unifying Lens question is the closest existing mechanism for solo practitioners but is currently optional.
