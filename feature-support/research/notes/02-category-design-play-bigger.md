## Framework Summary

Category Design is a business strategy discipline from the book *Play Bigger* (2016) by Al Ramadan, Dave Peterson, Christopher Lochhead, and Kevin Maney. Core premise: **the most successful companies don't compete in existing categories — they create entirely new ones.** The company that creates and defines a new category becomes the "Category King" and captures 76% of the market cap (per HBR research cited in the book).

Category Design is described as working the "magic triangle" of **product, company, and category** simultaneously.

## Key Concepts

### The Problem as Proxy for the Category
"The problem is the proxy for the category and is the strategic element you see missing in the world." Category design starts with identifying an unsolved problem — not building a better solution to an existing one.

### FroTos (From/To)
The journey you want customers to take from their current paradigm to the new one. Category designers reframe how people think about the current state. Example: Salesforce invented the term "on-premise software" to make the existing paradigm sound outdated, creating contrast with their cloud-based approach.

### The Point of View (POV)
A powerful, provocative statement that: frames the category problem, explains ramifications of not solving it, presents your vision for the future, and declares your unique answer. The POV is designed to cause an "aha moment" that changes perception.

### The Category Blueprint & Ecosystem
A visual map of people, processes, and products required to fulfill the category potential. Shows both what exists today and what will be built.

### Lightning Strike
A concentrated marketing execution that "conditions the world to see what you see." Targets cognitive biases of the category ecosystem to anchor your agenda and drive conformity. All company functions work together in a concentrated period.

### Internal Mobilization
Unifying the entire company around the category vision. "Category design is part of company culture."

## The Process (from Play Bigger)

1. **Identify the problem** and your unique technology/market insight
2. **Define the solution & category name** — comprised of market type, nature of problem, and audience
3. **Create the category blueprint & ecosystem** — visual map of the full category
4. **Develop the POV** — the provocative story that reframes the world
5. **Internal mobilization** — align the company
6. **Lightning strike** — concentrated market-conditioning campaign
7. **Flywheel** — ongoing category development and evolution

### Five Golden Rules of Category Design:
1. Fight the status quo, not the competition
2. Understand who the category is for, and who it isn't
3. See problems others don't or can't
4. Solve with a vision for the future
5. Frame it. Name it. Claim it.

## Key Quotes

- "Category design is about creating a powerful and provocative story that causes customers to make a choice. The story evokes something different from what came before, not just better."
- "You can position yourself, or you can be positioned."
- "Category design is marketing, PR, and advertising when it is all focused on conditioning the market to desire and need whatever you're giving it."

## Relevance to Brand Compass Benchmarking

### Where Category Design Challenges Brand Compass

1. **Category creation vs. competitive positioning:** Brand Compass Phase 3 asks "What do you believe that practitioners often disagree with?" and "What mainstream advice misses the point?" — these are contrarian POV questions. But Category Design goes further: it asks whether the client should be **creating an entirely new category** rather than positioning within an existing one. Phase 3's positioning statement ("For [audience], I'm the [category] who...") assumes an existing category. Category Design asks: should you invent the category itself?

2. **Category naming as a discipline:** Play Bigger treats naming the category as a critical strategic step. Brand Compass doesn't explicitly ask clients to name their market category or consider whether they should create a new one. Phase 3's "What word or phrase do you want associated with your name?" and "What concept do you want to be known for?" are adjacent but not the same as category naming.

3. **The POV as a deliverable:** Category Design's POV is a specific artifact: it frames the problem, explains consequences, presents vision, and declares the answer. Brand Compass's "contrarian POV" from Phase 3 captures the belief, but doesn't structure it as a complete Problem → Consequences → Vision → Answer document. The Category Design POV is more rigorous.

4. **FroTos / reframing the status quo:** Category Design explicitly reframes the existing paradigm to make it seem outdated. Brand Compass's positioning work positions the client as different/better, but doesn't include a structured exercise for reframing the competition's paradigm. The "Most experts say ____. I say ____" question (Phase 3 Q16) approaches this but is simpler than a full FroTo exercise.

5. **Ecosystem thinking:** Category Design maps the full ecosystem (customers, competitors, partners, analysts, media). Brand Compass maps customers deeply but doesn't map the broader ecosystem.

### Where Brand Compass Is Stronger

1. Brand Compass works for any entity type (solo creator, personal brand, org); Category Design is oriented toward companies with products/services
2. Brand Compass covers personality, voice, visual identity — dimensions Category Design doesn't address
3. Brand Compass's 8-phase process is more accessible; Category Design requires significant organizational commitment

### Where Brand Compass Aligns

1. Phase 3's contrarian POV questions align with Category Design's emphasis on challenging the status quo
2. Phase 3's "intersection" concept (combining worlds) can be a foundation for category creation
3. Phase 1's core belief provides the raw material for a Category Design POV

- **COMPLICATED:** Brand Compass's Phase 3 does positioning within existing categories; Category Design pushes toward category creation. Both are valid, but Brand Compass could benefit from at least asking the question: "Should you create a new category?"
- **GAP:** No explicit category naming exercise
