## Key Findings

### 1. Standard Architecture Models

The canonical framework is Aaker & Joachimsthaler's **Brand Relationship Spectrum** (Brand Leadership, 1999). A continuous spectrum, not discrete boxes, but practitioners describe four positions:

| Model | Description | Best Fit | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Branded House** | One master brand, all offerings under it | Related offerings, limited budget, strong master brand | Google, Apple, FedEx |
| **Sub-Brand** | Master brand + distinct sub-brand value props | Different segments but shared parent equity | Honda Accord, Starbucks Reserve |
| **Endorsed Brand** | Independent sub-brands with visible parent endorsement | New offerings needing borrowed credibility | Mini by BMW |
| **House of Brands** | Fully independent brands, parent invisible | Unrelated offerings, incompatible audiences | P&G, Unilever, Alphabet |
| **Hybrid** | Multiple models with explicit rules per portfolio segment | Large mature companies | Coca-Cola |

Key insight (Vivaldi/Aaker): Models are positions on a spectrum. Sophisticated companies occupy multiple positions based on customer decision logic, not org-chart convenience.

### 2. When Architecture Gets Addressed

**Consensus: architecture is a strategy-layer decision before identity work, alongside or immediately after positioning.**

The Branding Journal's 5-stage model positions it in Stage 2 (Brand Strategy Development). Marketing MO: "Brand architecture is your brand strategy foundation — it sets the foundation before personality, messaging, promise, and visual requirements."

**Sequencing:**
1. Context analysis / competitive audit
2. Positioning
3. **Architecture** ← here
4. Identity / messaging / visual
5. Execution

**Practical triggers:**
- Stage 1 (single offering): not a decision yet
- **Stage 2 (second or third offering added): critical decision point** — Spellbrand: worst mistakes happen here
- Also review at: major strategic shifts, acquisitions, market expansion, every 2-3 years (Jacob Tyler)

**Siegel+Gale diagnostic triggers** (symptoms architecture isn't working):
- Customers confused about what you offer
- Customers unaware of full extent of offerings
- Customers don't understand how brands connect
- Business vision and budget misaligned with current model

### 3. Solo Practitioners with Multiple Offerings

**Under-documented in literature.** Standard architecture canon built for corporate portfolios, not individuals.

**Default for solos: Branded House, intentionally managed.**
- Personal brand IS the master brand
- Offerings (courses, coaching, podcast, consulting) are descriptive sub-offerings
- Branded House concentrates equity — every piece of content feeds the same brand
- House of Brands requires funding independent marketing per brand — solos almost never have that capacity

**Solo practitioner patterns:**

| Scenario | Recommended | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Consultant + coaching + podcast | Branded House | Same audience, expertise, voice |
| Personal brand + B2B agency | Endorsed / light Sub-brand | Agency serves different buyers |
| Course creator with unrelated audiences | Endorsed / Sub-brand per audience | Incompatible buyers need separation |
| Personal brand + physical product | Sub-brand or House of Brands only if very different audience | Depends on buyer overlap |

**The naming trap:** Most common mistake is creating product names that become de facto sub-brands without deciding that's what they are. "The Clarity Method" + "Clarity Cast" + "Clarity Intensive" = accidental themed House of Brands with no anchoring parent brand.

### 4. Failure Modes When Architecture Is Skipped

**Customer-facing:**
- Offer confusion — can't understand how offerings relate
- Incomplete awareness — cross-sell breaks down
- Trust dilution — failure in one offering contaminates others
- Positioning blur — contradictory messaging across offerings

**Internal/operational:**
- Architecture entropy — new offerings decided case-by-case, portfolio becomes incoherent
- Resource dilution — marketing spread across multiple underfunded brands
- Internal misalignment — team can't explain or cross-sell naturally

**Strategic:**
- Missed leverage — equity in one offering doesn't transfer to others
- Growth ceiling — portfolio can't absorb new offerings cleanly
- Tactical substitution — fixing logos/names instead of structural problem

Branding Strategy Insider: architecture is frequently misused as internal structure solution rather than outward-facing strategic tool.

## Relevance to Brand Compass

**Current state:** No explicit architecture step. Phase 3 covers positioning and service definition but doesn't ask "How do these offerings relate to each other and to the master brand?"

**Where it surfaces:** Architecture questions are latent in Phase 3 (positioning) and Phase 0 (entity type). For solo practitioners with multiple offerings, most naturally raised at transition between Phase 0 and Phase 1, or during Phase 3 service definition.

**Suggested trigger:** If client discloses multiple distinct offerings in Phase 0 (or entity type is business/agency), an architecture framing question belongs in Phase 3 after positioning but before messaging. Shapes which offerings get named independently, how master brand relates to them, and what Visual Director needs to account for.

**Additional note:** The solo practitioner architecture question is genuinely under-served by existing literature — Brand Compass could address it more systematically than anything currently available.

**Finding tag:** GAP — No brand architecture step. For multi-offer clients, this is a structural omission. For single-offer clients, it's a non-issue. A conditional trigger based on offer complexity would address this without adding unnecessary weight to simple engagements.
