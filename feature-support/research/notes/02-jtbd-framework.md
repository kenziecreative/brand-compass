## Framework Summary

Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) is an outcome-driven innovation framework. Core premise: **customers don't buy products — they "hire" products to get "jobs" done.** Conceptualized by Tony Ulwick in 1990, popularized by Clayton Christensen in *The Innovator's Solution* (2003).

The framework shifts focus from product features and customer demographics to **the underlying progress customers are trying to make in their lives.**

## Three Types of Jobs

### 1. Functional Jobs
The practical task the customer needs to accomplish. The most straightforward type. "I need to get from A to B." "I need to file my taxes."

### 2. Emotional Jobs
How the customer wants to feel during and after the experience. "I want to feel confident about my financial future." "I want to feel creative and inspired."

### 3. Social Jobs
How the customer wants to be perceived by others. "I want to be seen as a responsible parent." "I want to be recognized as an expert."

## The JTBD Statement Formula

> "When [situation/circumstance], I want to [job/motivation], so I can [desired outcome] without [pain point]."

Example: "When I feel overwhelmed by work, I want quick healthy meal options, so I can stay productive without sacrificing health."

## The Switch Interview (Bob Moesta Method)

The "switch interview" is the primary JTBD research methodology. It identifies the **exact moment and circumstances** when a customer decided to seek out a solution.

### Structure:
1. **The trigger event** (~10 min): When did you first start looking? What made the status quo unacceptable at that specific moment?
2. **Understanding their world** (~2 min): Role, responsibilities, what "normal" looked like before
3. **Pinpointing the first thought** (~3 min): The moment they realized the current situation wasn't working
4. **The search process**: What alternatives did they consider? What criteria did they use?
5. **The decision**: What tipped the balance? What anxieties did they have about switching?
6. **Post-purchase**: Did the product deliver on the job?

### Four Forces of Progress:
- **Push:** Dissatisfaction with current situation
- **Pull:** Attraction to new solution
- **Anxiety:** Fear of change/new solution
- **Habit:** Comfort with current approach

A switch happens when Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit.

## JTBD Applied to Brand Messaging

From Sivo Insights / Fullstory:

1. Start with clear JTBD statements
2. Identify functional AND emotional benefits
3. Map each job to messaging that addresses both
4. Use customer language (verbatim from interviews) in messaging
5. "By aligning a JTBD with a brand, the customer reduces their need to think. The stronger the association between a brand and a job, the harder it is for competitors to move in on your business."

JTBD-driven messaging formula: "You have a problem. Our product solves it by doing [X] better than any other product."

## Relevance to Brand Compass Benchmarking

### Where JTBD Challenges Brand Compass

1. **Functional/emotional/social job taxonomy:** Brand Compass Phase 2 asks about audience struggles, trigger moments, and transformations, but doesn't systematically separate functional, emotional, and social dimensions. The questions "What are they struggling with?" and "What transformation are they seeking?" are open-ended — they may or may not elicit all three job types. JTBD would structure this explicitly.

2. **The switch moment / trigger specificity:** Brand Compass Phase 2 asks "What moment triggers them to seek help?" (Q7) — this is the JTBD trigger question. But JTBD goes much deeper: what made the status quo unacceptable *at that specific moment*? What alternatives did they consider? What anxieties did they have about switching? Brand Compass captures the trigger but not the full switching context (four forces of progress).

3. **Customer language for messaging:** JTBD emphasizes using the customer's exact words in messaging. Brand Compass's Passive Voice Capture and Voice Analyzer capture the *client's* voice, not the *customer's* voice. The messaging in Phase 5 is crafted from the client's perspective ("what's your origin story?"), not from customer interview data.

4. **Outcome framing vs. identity framing:** JTBD frames everything in terms of outcomes the customer wants to achieve. Brand Compass is primarily identity-framing: who is the brand, what does it believe, how does it show up. These are complementary but different lenses. Brand Compass could be stronger if it paired identity-based brand outputs with outcome-based customer messaging.

### Where Brand Compass Aligns

1. Phase 2's "Market of One" exercise captures a specific person with specific struggles — this is adjacent to JTBD's specificity
2. Phase 2 Q7 ("What moment triggers them to seek help?") is a JTBD switch trigger question
3. Phase 3's positioning statement format ("For [audience], I'm the [category] who [does what] so they can [outcome]") uses outcome language

### Where Brand Compass Is Stronger

1. Brand Compass captures identity, personality, voice, visual — dimensions JTBD doesn't address
2. Brand Compass produces a complete brand system; JTBD produces customer insights that inform product/messaging decisions
3. Brand Compass has a structured facilitation process; JTBD is a research methodology, not a brand-building process

### Finding Tags

- **GAP:** No explicit functional/emotional/social job taxonomy in audience discovery
- **COMPLICATED:** Trigger moment question exists (Phase 2 Q7) but lacks JTBD's depth on switching forces (push, pull, anxiety, habit)
- **GAP:** Customer voice (how the audience talks about their problem) is not systematically captured — only client voice is
- **COMPLICATED:** Brand Compass is identity-framed; JTBD is outcome-framed. Both are valid but pairing them would strengthen messaging
