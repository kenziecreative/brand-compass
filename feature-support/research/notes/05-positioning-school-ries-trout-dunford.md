## Key Findings

### Ries/Trout: Classic Positioning Theory (1981)

Core principles:
1. **Positioning happens in the prospect's mind**, not in the product — "Positioning is what you do to the mind of the intended prospect"
2. **Own a word** — the brand should own a single concept in the prospect's mind (Volvo = safety, FedEx = overnight)
3. **Find a window** — position against what already exists in the mind, not in a vacuum
4. **Define what you're NOT** — often more powerful than defining what you are (7-Up's "uncola," Avis's "We're #2, We Try Harder")
5. **Line extension trap** — extending a strong brand name to new categories dilutes the original position (Dial soap → Dial deodorant)
6. **First-mover advantage** — being first in a category is the most powerful position; if not first, create a new category
7. **Repositioning the competition** — explicitly reframe how prospects think about the leader (Apple's "I'm a Mac" campaign)

Key tactical insight: "words don't contain meanings. The meanings are not in the words. They are in the people using the words."

### April Dunford: Obviously Awesome (2019, 2nd ed. 2026)

Dunford's 5-component positioning methodology:
1. **Competitive Alternatives** — what customers would use if you didn't exist (start here, not with your product)
2. **Unique Attributes** — features/capabilities you have that alternatives lack
3. **Value (and proof)** — benefits those features enable for customers, with evidence
4. **Target Market Characteristics** — characteristics of buyers who care most about your value
5. **Market Category** — the market frame that makes your value obvious

(+Bonus: Relevant Trends)

Critical insight: **sequence matters**. Must start with competitive alternatives and work forward. Starting with market category (as traditional positioning statements do) assumes the answer before doing the work.

Dunford explicitly critiques the traditional positioning statement ("For [target], [brand] is the [category] that [differentiator]...") as:
1. It conflates the components of positioning into a single output
2. It assumes there's only one answer for each blank
3. It creates a false sense of "done" — positioning needs ongoing refinement

### Dunford vs. Ries/Trout

| Dimension | Ries/Trout | Dunford |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Starting point | Prospect's mind | Competitive alternatives |
| Core question | "What word do you own?" | "What would customers use instead?" |
| Category approach | Own position in existing category, or create new | Market category is a *choice* that frames value |
| Method | Intuitive/strategic | 5-step structured exercise with team |
| Output | Position in mind | Positioning as 5 interlocking components |
| Era | Mass media, limited channels | Multi-channel, SaaS, crowded markets |

### Relevance to Brand Compass

Brand Compass Phase 3 asks:
- "Complete: For [audience], I'm the [category] who [does what differently] so they can [outcome]" — this IS the traditional positioning statement Dunford critiques
- Territory and "own a word" questions align with Ries/Trout
- Contrarian POV aligns with "define what you're NOT" and repositioning

What's missing relative to Ries/Trout:
- **Explicit competitive framing** — Phase 3 asks about differentiation but doesn't systematically ask "what do prospects currently use/do instead?" The Research Analyst agent covers this partially, but it's optional.
- **Word ownership test** — Phase 3 asks "what word or phrase do you want associated with your name?" but doesn't test whether it's *available* or already owned by a competitor

What's missing relative to Dunford:
- **Competitive alternatives as starting point** — Phase 3 doesn't explicitly ask "what would your customers do if you didn't exist?" This is different from "who are your competitors?"
- **Value proof requirement** — Phase 3 captures differentiators but doesn't require evidence that customers actually care about them
- **Market category as a deliberate choice** — Phase 3 doesn't ask "what market category best frames your value?" (also identified in Phase 3 pipeline analysis)

What Brand Compass does BETTER than both:
- **Deeper personality and voice integration** — neither Ries/Trout nor Dunford address personality, archetypes, or voice as positioning elements
- **Origin/belief foundation** — Phase 1's core belief work goes deeper than either framework's "why" question
- **Emotional territory** — Brand Compass claims emotional/philosophical territory, not just market position
