## Key Findings — Do/Don't Examples

### Finding 1: Do/Don't examples are considered best practice THROUGHOUT brand guidelines, not just for logos

Multiple authoritative sources explicitly state that do/don't examples should accompany every major brand element — logo, color, typography, imagery, and voice/tone.

- **Frontify** (19 brand guidelines review): "Many teams add 'dos and don'ts' for different file types or show use cases." Specifically praises Tonies for containing "many practical examples showing how (not) to use the brand elements" and Cash App for "extensive 'dos and don'ts' to maintain a clean and instantly recognizable visual identity."
- **Kedraco** (2026 brand guidelines guide): Lists as a top best practice: "Use both 'do' and 'don't' examples for clarity" across all guideline sections.
- **Magnt** (2026 ultimate guide): Identifies "Mistake #3: No examples of wrong usage" as a common brand guideline failure. Solution: "Show both correct and incorrect examples for each rule."
- **Together Agency**: Specifically calls out imagery as needing do/don't treatment: "Provide visual examples, dos and don'ts, and explain the rationale behind the chosen style to maintain visual integrity across all media."
- **Wells Mountain Initiative / brand guidelines best practices PDF**: "Where possible, you should include examples of 'dos and don'ts' alongside each component so that employees can see what is and isn't on-brand."
- **IxDF (Interaction Design Foundation)**: Lists do/don't examples for logo usage, color palette, typography, and imagery as standard guideline components.

**Tag: SUPPORTED** — Do/don't examples are an industry-wide best practice across all brand guideline sections.

### Finding 2: Logo misuse remains the most common and most detailed do/don't section

While the principle extends everywhere, logo misuse pages are by far the most developed in practice. Typical logo don'ts include: stretching/squeezing, unauthorized color changes, placing on clashing backgrounds, removing elements, using below minimum size, overprinting, and using as a background pattern.

- **University of Arkansas brand guidelines**: Dedicated "What Not to Do" page with visual examples of logo misuse (stretching, unauthorized colors, separating elements, overprinting).
- **Identity Creative**: Lists "dos and don'ts" as one of the "7 Logo Laws" — a dedicated section in any brand identity standards guide.
- **Tailor Brands / VistaPrint**: Both show logo do/don't sections as standard, with visual examples of incorrect color combinations, dimensions, fonts, spacing.

**Tag: SUPPORTED** — Logo do/don'ts are the most mature and universally expected section. Other sections (color, type, imagery, voice) increasingly include them but with less depth.

### Finding 3: Voice/tone do/don'ts are emerging as a standard practice

Voice guidelines increasingly use a do/don't format, though they're newer and less standardized than visual do/don'ts.

- **The Lane Agency**: Recommends "dos and don'ts" as a core component of tone of voice guidelines: "Some simple dos and don'ts are a quick way to cover off major copy characteristics and practices." Can be structured per voice pillar or as one central list.
- **Branded Agency**: Recommends a voice chart with columns: "Trait, description, do's and do nots."
- **Uber**: Provides voice guidelines with core characteristics and examples of how to sound (and not sound) like the brand.
- **Channel 4**: Describes voice as "bold, surprising, and challenging" with headline examples showing correct application.
- **Sally Tyson (LinkedIn)**: Lists imagery direction with "Do's and don'ts for visuals" as a standard brand deliverable item for small businesses.

**Tag: SUPPORTED** — Voice/tone do/don'ts are now considered standard, not optional. They are less visually demonstrable than logo/color misuse but follow the same structural principle.

### Finding 4: The "why" behind do/don'ts matters more than the rule itself

Multiple sources emphasize that effective do/don't examples include rationale, not just prohibition.

- **Together Agency**: "Make sure your guide explains the 'why' behind each rule. When people understand the reasoning, they're more likely to follow the rules with intention rather than obligation."
- **brandguide.io**: "Simply listing a brand asset is not enough. Graphic designers need those guidelines to be able to represent your brand properly. Include instructions on how and when the asset is to be used."

**Tag: SUPPORTED** — Rationale-backed do/don'ts are more effective than bare rules. This is a quality marker, not just a formatting preference.

### Summary Answer — Topic A

Do/don't examples are considered essential throughout brand guidelines — logo, color, typography, imagery, and voice — not just for logos. However, **the depth and visual explicitness scales by element type**: logos have the most developed do/don't libraries (10-20 specific misuse examples is common), color and typography have moderate treatment (approved combinations, forbidden pairings), imagery has emerging treatment (style examples, mood mismatches), and voice/tone has the least visual but still uses the format (trait tables with do/don't columns). For Brand Compass, this means the Visual System and UI Kit outputs should include do/don't specimens for each major element, and the Voice Guide should include do/don't examples for each voice trait.

## Key Findings — Iconography & Graphic Devices

### Finding 5: Custom iconography systems are an enterprise/product-tier deliverable, NOT standard for solopreneurs or small businesses

The sources draw a clear line between who needs custom icon systems and who doesn't.

- **Enterprise/product examples**: Adobe Spectrum, PTC, HBS, and Duolingo all maintain detailed icon systems with pixel grids, stroke weights, compound icon rules, and usage guidelines. These are design-system-level investments.
- **Adobe Design**: Icon systems are described as part of a "design system's iconography" that "creates a unified brand experience across a product portfolio." This is explicitly product/platform language.
- **HBS Identity Guidelines**: Uses Google Material Icons as their icon library rather than building fully custom icons — even Harvard outsources to an existing system and customizes application rules.
- **Active Marketing**: Notes that "brands with refined icon systems experience up to 35% higher engagement" but frames this in B2B, SaaS, and healthcare contexts — not solopreneur or small business.
- **VistaPrint**: Advises small businesses to "Launch with the essentials: logo, color palette, fonts and brand voice, and add more branding elements like your tagline, shapes, imagery and positioning as you grow." Icons are not in the essentials list.
- **Design Magazine Australia**: The "Essential Brand Package" for small businesses includes logo systems, color palettes, typography, brand documentation, and implementation rules. No mention of custom iconography.

**Tag: SUPPORTED** — Custom icon systems are enterprise/product deliverables. Small businesses and solopreneurs do not need or expect them. When icons are needed, using an existing library (Google Material Icons, Phosphor, etc.) with brand-specific application rules is the practical approach.

### Finding 6: Graphic devices (patterns, textures, supporting graphics) ARE increasingly expected — even for small brands

Unlike custom icon systems, graphic devices (patterns, textures, brand shapes, supporting graphics) are becoming a standard tier of brand deliverables, even at the small-business level.

- **Tenet (2026 visual branding guide)**: Lists "Graphic Elements and Patterns" as element #5 of visual identity (after logo, color, typography, imagery). Describes them as "supporting visuals such as icons, textures, backgrounds, shapes, and patterns that complement the core brand elements." Cites Duolingo as an example.
- **The Solopreneur Society**: Dedicates an entire article to "Brand Pattern Suites" for solopreneurs. Recommends 1-3 patterns per brand. "A pattern suite is simply a set of patterns (or textures) that reinforce your personality and the atmosphere your brand lives in. The customizable nature of the subject matter and colors you use oftentimes make a pattern suite more recognizable than a logo suite."
- **noissue (brand elements guide)**: "Graphics — elements such as illustrations, patterns, icons, and textures pull everything together to create a cohesive brand identity. Patterns and textures can be used for packaging, social media backgrounds, website elements and more."
- **yourmarketingbff.com**: "I love using illustrations or brand icons to create wallpaper, use as wrapping, or as a background to some of your marketing materials (business cards, menu, tags, product shots, etc)."
- **Sally Tyson (LinkedIn brand deliverables)**: Lists "Imagery Direction" including "Icon style" and "Illustration direction" as standard deliverables, with "Do's and don'ts for visuals."
- **Jenny Henderson Studio**: Brand guidelines for small business clients include approved color combinations and typography styles as standard — patterns and graphic elements are treated as a next-level-up deliverable.
- **Bastyr University**: Even a small institution maintains branded textures and patterns (hexagon, sprout, etc.) with specific usage guidelines.

**Tag: SUPPORTED** — Graphic devices (patterns, textures, brand shapes) are increasingly standard even for small brands. They're not enterprise-only. The level of sophistication scales: solopreneurs might have 1-3 patterns; enterprises have full libraries with grid systems.

### Finding 7: Illustration systems are a premium/specialized deliverable

Full illustration systems (character sets, scene builders, custom illustration libraries) remain a premium offering, distinct from simple graphic devices.

- **Kamilla Illustration**: Prices illustration-based branding packages from $1,200 (Essentials: 1 logo + 1 illustration) to $5,000 (Ultimate: full illustration suite). This positions custom illustration as a paid premium add-on.
- **QED42**: Reviews illustration systems (DrawKit, Blush, Hey!) as tools for design teams — positioning them as team/product tools, not standard small-business deliverables.
- **designsystems.com**: Treats illustration as a design-system component alongside iconography — product-level infrastructure.
- **Adobe Design**: Lists "illustration, motion, and 3D" as principles that "can be temporarily set aside to focus on what's foundational."

**Tag: SUPPORTED** — Full illustration systems are premium/enterprise deliverables. For Brand Compass clients, illustration *direction* (style, subjects, AI prompts) is appropriate; a full illustration *library* is not.

### Summary Answer — Topic B

For Brand Compass's typical clients (solopreneurs, small businesses, startups):

1. **Custom iconography systems**: NOT expected. These are enterprise/product deliverables. Brand Compass should NOT include a custom icon system as a standard output. If the client needs icons, recommend an existing library (e.g., Phosphor, Heroicons) with brand-specific application rules (color, size, style preferences).

2. **Graphic devices (patterns, textures, brand shapes)**: YES, increasingly expected even at the small-business tier. Brand Compass should include 1-3 brand patterns or supporting graphic elements as part of the Visual Identity System output. This is achievable with AI generation and adds significant brand recognition value.

3. **Illustration systems**: NOT expected as standard. Illustration *direction* (style guidance, subject matter, AI generation prompts) is appropriate. A full illustration *library* is a premium add-on beyond Brand Compass scope.
