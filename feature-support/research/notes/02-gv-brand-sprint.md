## Framework Summary

The GV Brand Sprint is a **3-hour compressed brand exercise** designed for startup teams. Created by Jake Knapp (creator of the 5-day Design Sprint) at Google Ventures. It consists of six exercises using a "note-and-vote" process with a designated "decider" (typically the CEO).

**Key distinction:** This is not a brand development process. It's a rapid alignment exercise — a "brand cheat sheet" for making decisions. It explicitly positions itself as "not a replacement for a good branding agency, but much better than nothing."

## The Six Exercises

### 1. 20-Year Roadmap (15 minutes)
Draw a timeline at 5, 10, 15, 20 years. Answer: "What will your company be doing at each of those future dates?" Note-and-vote for a single answer per milestone.

**What this captures:** Long-term vision and ambition beyond the current product.

### 2. What, How, Why (30 minutes)
- **What:** What does your company do? (Products/services)
- **How:** How do you do it? (Differentiating method)
- **Why:** Why do you do it? (Purpose beyond profit — e.g., "promote healthy living")

Note-and-vote for each.

**What this captures:** Golden Circle (Sinek) applied in sprint format.

### 3. Top 3 Values (30 minutes)
List company values (e.g., Honesty, Integrity, Sustainability, Affordability, Luxury). Note-and-vote to narrow to three. Decider ranks them and identifies the single most important value.

**What this captures:** Value hierarchy and prioritization.

### 4. Top 3 Audiences (30 minutes)
"Audience" extends beyond customers to anyone whose opinion matters: other businesses, reporters, advertisers, employees, potential hires, regulators. Note-and-vote to narrow to top three.

**What this captures:** Stakeholder mapping beyond customer segments.

### 5. Personality Sliders (20 minutes)
Position company between pairs of brand extremes:
- "Friend" ←→ "Authority"
- "Young & Innovative" ←→ "Mature & Classic"
- "Playful" ←→ "Serious"
- "Mass Appeal" ←→ "Elite"

Everyone marks positions. Discuss disagreements. Decider finalizes.

**What this captures:** Brand personality as a spectrum, not discrete traits.

### 6. Competitive Landscape (30 minutes)
2x2 matrix: "Classic" to "Modern" (x-axis), "Expressive" to "Reserved" (y-axis). Plot competitors, then plot your own company. Discuss how positioning aligns with previous exercises.

**What this captures:** Relative competitive positioning as spatial relationship.

## Output

A "brand cheat sheet" — photos of whiteboards from each exercise compiled into a slide deck. Used for making decisions about naming, logo, messaging, hiring.

## Process Design

- 2-6 participants, must include company executives
- Designated "decider" (CEO) has final call
- "Note-and-vote" prevents groupthink: individual writing → read aloud → vote → discuss → decider chooses
- Good triggers for running: naming the company, designing a logo, hiring an agency, writing a manifesto
- "Only run a Brand Sprint if you really have to. If you won't use the results right away, wait for a trigger event."

## Relevance to Brand Compass Benchmarking

### Where GV Sprint Challenges Brand Compass

1. **20-Year Roadmap exercise:** Brand Compass has no explicit long-term vision exercise beyond the mission/vision questions in Phase 1 (which are only asked for org entities). The 20-year roadmap forces specificity about where the brand is going over time — a temporal dimension Brand Compass doesn't capture.

2. **Stakeholder mapping beyond customers:** GV's "Top 3 Audiences" explicitly includes employees, reporters, regulators, investors — not just customers. Brand Compass Phase 2 focuses exclusively on customers/clients. For businesses, secondary audiences (investors, press, employees) shape brand perception but aren't captured.

3. **Personality sliders (spectrum, not discrete):** GV uses continuous spectrums between polar opposites. Brand Compass Phase 4 asks for "4-6 adjectives" and uses archetype mapping. The slider approach captures tensions and positions; the adjective approach captures qualities. They're complementary, not redundant — sliders reveal where you sit between extremes, adjectives describe how you show up.

4. **Competitive landscape as spatial exercise:** GV's 2x2 matrix is a visual positioning tool. Brand Compass Phase 3 asks about differentiation and territory but doesn't include a structured competitive mapping exercise. The Research Analyst agent is optional and triggered passively.

5. **Process design for alignment:** GV is designed for multi-stakeholder alignment (note-and-vote, decider role). Brand Compass is a 1:1 conversation between facilitator and client. For org clients with multiple decision-makers, Brand Compass may not adequately handle alignment challenges.

### Where Brand Compass Is Stronger

1. **Depth:** GV is deliberately shallow — 3 hours for the entire brand. Brand Compass goes vastly deeper on every dimension.
2. **Discovery quality:** GV uses note-and-vote quick answers; Brand Compass uses extended conversational discovery with follow-up and challenge.
3. **Deliverables:** GV produces a "cheat sheet"; Brand Compass produces 8+ professional documents.
4. **Voice, messaging, visual:** GV doesn't touch these at all.

### Finding Tags

- **GAP:** Brand Compass lacks a long-term vision/roadmap exercise (temporal dimension)
- **GAP:** No stakeholder mapping beyond customers (employees, press, investors as brand audiences)
- **COMPLICATED:** Personality sliders (spectrum) and personality traits (discrete adjectives) capture different things — Brand Compass could benefit from both
- **GAP:** No structured competitive mapping exercise (2x2 or equivalent)
- **COMPLICATED:** Multi-stakeholder alignment process absent from Brand Compass (fine for solo practitioners, potential gap for org clients)
