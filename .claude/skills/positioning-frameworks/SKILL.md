# Positioning Frameworks

## The Core Question

Positioning answers: "Why you, specifically?"

Not "why this category" or "why this type of solution" — why THIS person/brand over all alternatives, including doing nothing.

---

## The Positioning Statement

**Template:**
For [target audience], [Brand] is the [category/frame] that [key differentiator] so they can [outcome/benefit].

**Example:**
For growth-stage founders who feel overwhelmed by operational chaos, Kenzie is the systems-minded advisor who translates complexity into clarity so they can scale without losing their soul.

**Common mistakes:**
- Too broad: "For everyone who wants to succeed..."
- Features, not outcomes: "...that offers comprehensive solutions..."
- No real differentiator: "...that provides excellent service..."

## The Intersection Model

Distinctiveness comes from combining things others keep separate.

**Formula:** X + Y (+ Z)

**Examples:**
- "Strategy rigor + creative intuition"
- "Technical depth + human-centered design + business acumen"
- "Wall Street precision + Main Street values"

**How to find your intersection:**
1. List all domains you have credibility in
2. Identify which combinations are rare
3. Test: Would others claim this same combination?

**The test:** If your intersection is common, it's not an intersection — it's table stakes.

## Territory Claiming

Territory is the word or phrase you want associated with your name.

**Good territory:**
- Specific enough to own
- Broad enough to matter
- Aligned with where the world is going

**Examples:**
- "Human-centered AI" (specific + timely)
- "Organic systems" (metaphor that frames everything)
- "Minimum Lovable Product" (coined concept)

**How to claim territory:**
1. Name it (if it doesn't have a name, give it one)
2. Define it (you set the terms)
3. Repeat it (consistently, across contexts)
4. Defend it (when others misuse or dilute it)

## Contrarian Point of View

A contrarian POV differentiates by disagreeing with accepted wisdom.

**Template:** "Most people believe X. I believe Y."

**Examples:**
- "Most people think productivity is about doing more. I believe it's about doing less, better."
- "The industry says move fast and break things. I say move thoughtfully and build things that last."

**Requirements for a good contrarian POV:**
1. **Actually contrarian.** Disagrees with something people really believe.
2. **Defensible.** You can make a case for it.
3. **Consequential.** If true, it changes how you work.
4. **Authentic.** You genuinely believe it.

**The test:** Does stating this POV create some tension? If everyone agrees, it's not contrarian.

## Category Design

Sometimes the right move isn't to position within an existing category but to create a new one.

**When to consider category creation:**
- Existing categories force you to compete on their terms
- You're doing something genuinely new
- The old category has negative associations

**How to create a category:**
1. **Name it.** The category needs a name people can say.
2. **Define the problem it solves.** Why does this category need to exist?
3. **Establish criteria.** What makes something qualify (or not)?
4. **Position yourself as the exemplar.** You define what good looks like.

**Warning:** Category creation is expensive. You have to educate the market. Only do it if existing categories truly don't work.

## The "Only" Statement

A forcing function for differentiation:

"We are the ONLY [category] that [unique approach] for [specific audience]."

If you can't complete this statement, you don't have a position — you have a description.

**Example:**
"We are the ONLY AI consultancy that teaches through building, not presenting, for small business owners who learn by doing."

---

## White Space Identification

White space is what no one in your market is saying or claiming.

**How to find it:**
1. List what competitors claim
2. Identify the patterns (what everyone says)
3. Look for gaps (what no one says)
4. Test which gaps are opportunities vs. third rails

**White space types:**
- **Topic white space:** No one talks about X
- **Position white space:** Everyone says Y, no one says Z
- **Audience white space:** No one speaks to this segment
- **Format white space:** Everyone delivers this way, no one delivers that way

## Permission

Positioning must be earned. "Permission" is what gives you the right to claim a position.

**Sources of permission:**
- Experience (you've done the work)
- Credentials (formal qualifications)
- Results (track record of outcomes)
- Proximity (you're close to the problem)
- Perspective (unique vantage point)

**The test:** If a stranger claimed your position, would you believe them? Why? That's your permission.
