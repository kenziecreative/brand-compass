# Story Structures

## The Origin Story Arc

Every brand has an origin story. The structure is consistent:

### The Arc

1. **Before:** What was the world/situation like before?
2. **Inciting incident:** What happened that changed everything?
3. **Realization:** What did you see or understand?
4. **Response:** What did you do about it?
5. **Now:** Where are you now as a result?

### Template (100-150 words)

"For years, I [context/background]. Then [inciting incident] — and I saw [realization]. That's when I [response/action]. Today, [current state/impact]. I believe [core belief], which is why I [current work] for [audience]."

### Example

"For years, I watched small business owners struggle with technology — not because they couldn't learn it, but because no one was teaching it in a way that made sense for how they actually work. Then AI arrived, and I saw the same pattern repeating: powerful tools, terrible guidance. That's when I started translating — taking what the technologists were building and making it useful for the people who needed it most. Today, I help over a million small business owners make sense of new technology without the jargon or the hype. I believe the future of work isn't about replacing humans with AI — it's about giving humans better tools. That's what I'm building."

## Tagline Formulas

### The Tension Tagline

Format: "[Unexpected pairing]" or "[Surprising combination]"

Examples:
- "Analog wisdom for a digital world"
- "Serious strategy. Human delivery."
- "Technical depth. Plain English."

Works when: Your brand embodies a productive tension.

### The Promise Tagline

Format: "[Outcome you enable]"

Examples:
- "Think clearly. Build confidently."
- "From chaos to clarity."
- "Scale without losing your soul."

Works when: The transformation you enable is clear.

### The Belief Tagline

Format: Statement of belief or principle

Examples:
- "Simple is hard. Simple is worth it."
- "Tools for people who make things."
- "Good enough isn't."

Works when: Your conviction is distinctive.

### The "Only We" Tagline

Format: Implicit claim of uniqueness

Examples:
- "The other way to [do X]."
- "Where [X] meets [Y]."
- "For the ones who [distinctive behavior]."

Works when: You're creating a category or distinct approach.

## Boilerplate Structures

### One Sentence

**Formula:** "[Name] helps [audience] [do what] so they can [outcome]."

**Example:** "Kenzie helps growth-stage founders make sense of complexity so they can build systems that scale."

**Variation:** "[Name] is [what you are] for [who], [distinctive approach]."

**Example:** "Kenzie is a systems-minded advisor for founders, translating operational chaos into sustainable clarity."

### One Paragraph (50-75 words)

**Structure:**
1. What you do and for whom (1 sentence)
2. The problem you address (1 sentence)
3. Your approach/what makes you different (1 sentence)
4. Credibility marker (1 sentence)

**Example:**
"Kenzie works with growth-stage founders to build systems that scale without sacrificing culture. Most scaling advice treats organizations like machines—Kenzie treats them like living systems that need the right conditions to thrive. Using a blend of strategic frameworks and hands-on building, Kenzie helps leaders create clarity from chaos. He's helped over 100 companies navigate the messy middle between startup and scale-up."

### Three Paragraphs (150-200 words)

**Structure:**
1. **Paragraph 1:** What you do, for whom, and the outcome (the offer)
2. **Paragraph 2:** Why you're different, your approach, the philosophy (the differentiator)
3. **Paragraph 3:** Background, credentials, proof points (the credibility)

## Elevator Pitches

### 10 Seconds (The Hook)

**Formula:** "I help [specific audience] [specific outcome]."

**Example:** "I help founders build systems that let them scale without burning out."

**Keys:**
- Specific enough to spark interest
- Outcome-focused, not method-focused
- Conversational — something you'd actually say

### 30 Seconds (The Context)

**Formula:**
1. Hook (10 seconds)
2. The problem (10 seconds)
3. The approach (10 seconds)

**Example:**
"I help founders build systems that let them scale without burning out. Most scaling advice treats companies like machines — add more process, more hierarchy, more control. But that kills what made them special. I work with founders to build organic systems — structures that grow with the company instead of constraining it."

### 2 Minutes (The Story)

**Formula:**
1. Hook (10 seconds)
2. The problem — expanded (20 seconds)
3. Why it matters / what's at stake (20 seconds)
4. Your approach — with example (30 seconds)
5. Proof point or result (20 seconds)
6. Invitation / what's next (20 seconds)

## The "Most People / I Believe" Structure

For articulating contrarian POV:

**Template:**
"Most people in [domain] believe [common wisdom].
I believe [your contrarian view].
Here's why: [supporting logic or evidence].
Which means: [implication for how you work]."

**Example:**
"Most people in management consulting believe you need extensive research before making recommendations. I believe the best answers often emerge from building, not studying. Here's why: real-world feedback teaches what surveys and interviews can't. Which means I don't deliver PowerPoints — I build with you, learn fast, and adjust."

## Messaging Hierarchy

### Levels of Message

| Level | Purpose | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Tagline | Capture essence in few words | "Analog wisdom for a digital world" |
| Positioning | State the unique claim | "The only AI consultancy that teaches through building" |
| Value prop | Promise the outcome | "Make sense of AI without the hype or jargon" |
| Proof points | Demonstrate credibility | "Helped 1M+ small businesses adopt technology" |
| Story | Create emotional connection | The origin story |

### Message Testing

Ask of EVERY piece of copy before including it:
1. **Is it true?** Can you back it up?
2. **Is it distinctive?** Would competitors say the same thing?
3. **Is it meaningful?** Does your audience care?
4. **Is it clear?** Would someone outside your field understand it on first read, with zero context?

**#4 is the most important.** Clever copy that requires context to decode is failed copy. The reader was not in the discovery session. They will not stop to figure out what a coined term means. Say it plainly first — then add personality.
