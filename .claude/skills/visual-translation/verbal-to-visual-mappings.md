# Verbal-to-Visual Translation Mappings

Quick-lookup reference for Phase 7 visual translation. Consult alongside
`.claude/skills/visual-translation/SKILL.md` for full context on color
psychology, typography pairing principles, and brand world translation.

These mappings are starting points — the facilitator should adapt based on
the specific brand tension, intersection, and territory defined in Phases 1-6.
Two brands with the same archetype will look different because their
personality traits, voice register, and brand world differ.

---

## Section 1: Voice Register to Visual Register

Map voice characteristics (from Phase 6 discovery) to visual treatment:

| Voice Register | Visual Register | Typography Tendency | Color Tendency | Layout Tendency |
|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Conversational / casual | Warm, approachable | Humanist sans, friendly weights | Warm, mid-saturation | Relaxed spacing, organic flow |
| Academic / analytical | Precise, structured | Transitional serif or geometric sans | Cool, muted | Grid-based, clear hierarchy |
| Provocative / edgy | High-contrast, unexpected | Bold display, tight tracking | Saturated or stark contrast | Asymmetric, tension-creating |
| Poetic / lyrical | Flowing, spacious | Elegant serif, light weights | Soft, atmospheric | Generous whitespace, vertical rhythm |
| Direct / no-nonsense | Clean, functional | Grotesque sans, medium weights | Neutral, limited palette | Minimal decoration, dense info |
| Warm authority | Grounded, confident | Slab serif or strong humanist | Deep, rich tones | Balanced structure with warmth |
| Playful / irreverent | Dynamic, colorful | Quirky display + clean body | Bright, varied | Unexpected compositions |
| Intimate / confessional | Soft, personal | Handwritten accents + readable body | Muted warm | Close, contained layouts |

**How to use:** After Phase 6, identify the client's dominant voice register from the Voice Summary. Use the corresponding row as a starting visual hypothesis — then check for tension with the archetype and personality traits. Conflicts are interesting: a "direct / no-nonsense" voice with a Lover archetype might produce a restrained visual system with precisely placed warmth.

## Section 2: Personality Trait to Color Family (Expanded)

Extend the SKILL.md personality-to-visual table with specific color family guidance:

| Personality Trait | Primary Color Family | Secondary Options | Avoid |
|-------------------|---------------------|-------------------|-------|
| Warm | Amber, terracotta, rust | Soft gold, warm cream | Cool blues, clinical whites |
| Authoritative | Navy, charcoal, deep forest | Gold accents, crisp white | Pastels, bright playful tones |
| Playful | Coral, electric blue, sunshine yellow | Lime, magenta | Dark muted palettes |
| Sophisticated | Slate, ivory, muted sage | Champagne, dusty rose | Neon, primary colors |
| Approachable | Teal, warm peach, soft green | Sky blue, light coral | Black-heavy, stark contrast |
| Bold | Deep red, electric purple, black | Hot orange, metallic | Washed out, pastel |
| Calm | Ocean blue, sage, lavender | Soft gray, cream | High-contrast, saturated |
| Precise | Steel blue, clean gray, white | Accent in brand color | Organic, earthy |
| Organic | Olive, terracotta, bark brown | Moss, sand, clay | Neon, synthetic |
| Innovative | Violet, teal, electric | Silver, neon accent | Traditional, conservative |

**How to use:** Identify 2-3 dominant personality traits from Phase 4. Look up their color families. Where families overlap, you have palette candidates. Where they conflict, you have a design tension to resolve — which trait dominates, and which provides accent energy? Cross-reference with brand world environment and voice register before finalizing.

## Section 3: Archetype to Design System Properties

Consolidate SKILL.md archetype expressions into design-system-ready guidance. These properties apply to UI components, interactive elements, and layout decisions:

| Archetype | Corner Radius | Shadow Style | Spacing | Animation |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|---------|-----------|
| Innocent | Fully rounded | Soft, diffuse | Generous | Gentle, floating |
| Everyman | Medium rounded | Subtle | Comfortable | Natural, understated |
| Hero | Sharp or minimal | Strong, directional | Tight, powerful | Dynamic, purposeful |
| Caregiver | Rounded | Warm, soft | Open, breathing | Smooth, reassuring |
| Explorer | Mixed | Natural, organic | Expansive | Flowing, directional |
| Rebel | None (sharp) | Harsh or none | Tight, dense | Abrupt, surprising |
| Lover | Soft curves | Elegant, layered | Intimate | Slow, sensual |
| Creator | Variable | Artistic, unique | Asymmetric | Expressive, varied |
| Jester | Rounded, playful | Bouncy, colored | Loose, uneven | Bouncy, energetic |
| Sage | Consistent, medium | Subtle, structured | Ordered, hierarchical | Measured, deliberate |
| Magician | Transforming | Luminous, shifting | Dynamic | Transformative, morphing |
| Ruler | Precise, minimal | Strong, premium | Structured, commanded | Stately, authoritative |

**How to use:** Use the primary archetype to establish the default design system behavior. For brands with two archetypes, the primary drives the dominant properties and the secondary creates variation — e.g., a Sage/Rebel brand might use Sage's ordered spacing and structured shadows but Rebel's tight corner radius and abrupt animation on key interactions.

**Cross-reference with motion questions (Q26-28 in Phase 7):** The client's answers to the motion questions confirm or override these archetype defaults. If a Rebel client says "I want things to feel smooth and easing," the client's intent wins — document it as an intentional departure from archetype convention.

## Quick Cross-Reference Notes

**When voice and archetype point different directions:**
Resolve by asking which element the audience experiences first. Digital-first brands often lead with voice (the words come before the visuals in a search result or email). Visual-first brands (fashion, product, physical space) lead with archetype. The entry point determines which mapping takes precedence.

**When personality traits conflict:**
"Bold" and "Calm" can coexist — a bold structural choice (black headline, strong grid) with a calm color palette (ocean blue, sage). The design system expresses both without compromise. Conflict between traits is often the brand tension — make it visible rather than averaging it away.

**When a client's gut instinct contradicts the mappings:**
The mappings are starting hypotheses, not conclusions. If a client with a Sage archetype is drawn to bright, unexpected color — explore why. Sometimes preference reveals something the discovery conversation didn't surface. Use the question: "What do you want your audience to feel when they first see this?" and work backward from the emotional target.
