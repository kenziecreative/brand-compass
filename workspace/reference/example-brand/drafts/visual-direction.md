# Baseline — Visual Direction

**Agent:** Visual Director
**Date:** 2026-02-10
**Status:** Draft for review
**Client:** Baseline (Accounting & Compliance for Tech Startups)

---

## 1. Visual Tension

**"Trusted authority + Startup energy"**

Baseline's visual identity must hold two ideas simultaneously: the gravitas of a firm you'd trust with your SOC 2 audit and the forward momentum of a company that actually understands how startups operate. Too far toward authority and we look like every other accounting firm — mahogany desks, serif fonts, muted everything. Too far toward startup energy and we lose credibility with the CFOs and compliance officers who need to trust our rigor.

The resolution lives in precision paired with warmth. Think: a perfectly structured data table rendered in a modern sans-serif with generous whitespace. The information is serious; the presentation respects your time and intelligence. Nothing is decorative for its own sake. Every visual element earns its place by clarifying, organizing, or reinforcing trust.

This is the visual equivalent of Baseline's voice: clear, precise, confident without being arrogant.

## 2. Color Palette

### Primary — Navy (#1B2B4B)
The foundation of the entire palette. This specific navy was chosen over darker alternatives (like #0D1B2A) because it reads as authoritative without being severe. It carries the weight of financial services credibility — this is a color that belongs on an audit report cover — while remaining warm enough to avoid the cold, institutional feeling of pure dark blue. Used for primary text, headers, navigation, and key UI surfaces. It says: we are serious about this work.

### Accent — Teal (#2A9D8F)
The primary differentiator in the palette. Teal bridges the gap between the trustworthiness of blue and the growth connotation of green — which makes it structurally perfect for a compliance firm that serves scaling companies. This specific value (#2A9D8F) has enough saturation to function as a true accent — it pops against navy and white — without veering into the neon territory that would undermine professional credibility. Used for CTAs, links, data highlights, progress indicators, and any moment where the brand needs to signal "forward motion." It says: we are modern and we are moving.

### Secondary — Slate (#64748B)
The workhorse. Slate handles all the secondary text, captions, metadata, subtle borders, and supporting UI elements that make up the majority of any real interface or document. This value sits precisely between warm and cool gray, which means it pairs cleanly with both the navy (cool-leaning) and the warm gray background (warm-leaning) without creating visual tension. It never competes for attention. It says: the details are handled.

### Emphasis — Coral (#E8725C)
The strategic surprise. In a palette dominated by cool blues and neutrals, coral provides the single warm accent that prevents the brand from feeling clinical. It's used sparingly and with purpose: alert states, key metrics, important callouts, featured statistics, and moments where the brand needs human warmth. This specific coral was chosen for its softness — it's assertive without being aggressive, unlike a pure red or orange. It aligns with the "approachable" dimension of the brand personality. It says: pay attention here, but don't worry.

### Background — White (#FFFFFF)
Clean, unambiguous, professional. White backgrounds are non-negotiable for a firm whose work product includes compliance reports, financial documents, and audit materials. White signals transparency and clarity — two qualities that are foundational to Baseline's value proposition. No off-whites, no blue-tinted whites. Pure white. It says: nothing is hidden.

### Soft Background — Warm Gray (#F8F7F5)
The environmental color. Used for page sections, card backgrounds, alternate table rows, and any surface that needs to be visually distinct from the primary white without introducing a new color. The warm undertone (slightly yellow rather than blue) is intentional — it prevents the overall brand experience from feeling sterile. This is the color of a well-lit, modern office with light wood surfaces. It says: professional doesn't have to mean cold.

## 3. Typography

### Headlines — Plus Jakarta Sans
**Rationale:** Plus Jakarta Sans is a geometric sans-serif with optical corrections that keep it feeling human at display sizes. Its letterforms are clean and modern without the coldness of purely geometric typefaces like Futura. The slightly rounded terminals and generous x-height give it a tech-native quality — this is a typeface that looks at home on a SaaS marketing page or a product dashboard. It carries authority at large sizes without the stiffness of traditional corporate serif faces. We're using Bold (700) for primary headlines and SemiBold (600) for subheads.

**Usage:** Page titles, section headers, hero text, presentation slides, feature callouts. Minimum size 20px in digital, 14pt in print.

### Body — Inter
**Rationale:** Inter was designed specifically for computer screens, which makes it the structurally correct choice for a brand that operates almost entirely in digital contexts — web, email, PDFs, dashboards, slide decks. Its tall x-height and open apertures ensure legibility at small sizes, which matters for the dense informational content that compliance and financial services demand. Inter is also the default or near-default for much of the modern tech ecosystem, which means it feels native to Baseline's audience. It doesn't call attention to itself. It just works.

**Usage:** Body copy, UI text, email, long-form content, report narratives. Regular (400) for body, Medium (500) for emphasis, SemiBold (600) for inline labels. Minimum 14px in digital, 10pt in print. Line height 1.5 for body text, 1.4 for UI.

### Data & Code — JetBrains Mono
**Rationale:** Baseline works with technical audiences who read code, review infrastructure configurations, and interpret structured data daily. JetBrains Mono is a monospace typeface designed for development environments, and using it for code snippets, data tables, financial figures, and compliance control identifiers (e.g., CC6.1, TSC A1.2) signals that Baseline speaks this language natively. It also provides clear visual differentiation — when a reader sees JetBrains Mono, they know they're looking at a precise value, not narrative text.

**Usage:** Code blocks, data tables, financial figures, compliance control IDs, terminal output, API references, numerical dashboards. Regular (400) for standard use, Bold (700) for highlighted values. Minimum 13px in digital.

## 4. Mark & Logo Direction

### Primary Concept — Geometric "B" with Integrated Baseline
The logomark is a geometric letter "B" constructed from clean, angular forms. The critical detail: the bottom horizontal stroke of the "B" extends slightly to the right and carries an upward angle — a subtle integrated reference to an upward-trending baseline (in both the financial chart sense and the literal brand name sense). This is not a dramatic swoosh or arrow. It's a 4-6 degree incline that reads as quiet confidence and forward trajectory. At small sizes, it simply reads as a well-constructed B. At larger sizes, the intentionality becomes clear.

### Wordmark-First Approach
Baseline is a new brand entering a market where name recognition matters more than symbol recognition. The default lockup leads with the wordmark — "BASELINE" set in Plus Jakarta Sans Bold, tracked slightly open (+20-30 units) — with the geometric B mark positioned to the left. The mark can separate from the wordmark for favicons, app icons, and social avatars, but for the first 18-24 months of market presence, the full lockup should be the default in all contexts.

### Lockup Specifications
- **Primary lockup:** Mark + Wordmark, horizontal arrangement, navy on white
- **Secondary lockup:** Mark + Wordmark, navy on warm gray
- **Reversed lockup:** Mark + Wordmark, white on navy
- **Mark only:** Geometric B, used only for established-context placements (favicon, app icon, social avatar, branded merchandise)
- **Minimum clear space:** Height of the "B" mark on all sides
- **Minimum reproduction size:** 24px height for mark only, 80px width for full lockup

## 5. Imagery Style

### Data Visualizations
Clean, purposeful charts and graphs rendered in the brand palette. Line charts with navy lines on white backgrounds. Teal accent for highlighted data points. Coral for alert thresholds or key metrics. No 3D effects, no gradients on chart elements, no decorative gridlines. Every mark on the chart should represent a real data point. Style reference: the clarity of a well-built analytics dashboard, not the decoration of an infographic.

### Environmental Photography
Glass-and-light-wood office aesthetic. Natural light. Clean desks with single monitors showing structured data. Small team meetings in bright rooms — 3 to 5 people, not crowded conference tables. The spaces feel modern, organized, and warm. Think: a Series B company's office that's been intentionally designed but not extravagantly. Plants are fine. Exposed brick is fine. But the dominant materials are glass, light wood, and white surfaces.

### Contextual Elements
Dashboard screenshots (styled, not raw). Terminal windows with clean output. Structured documents with visible hierarchy. Checklist interfaces with completed items. These elements reinforce Baseline's technical fluency and the tangible outputs of their work — they show what "compliance infrastructure" actually looks like in practice.

### People (When Used)
Real-feeling, not performed. People working, not posing. Diverse teams in casual-professional attire. No suits unless the context specifically demands it (e.g., board meeting scenario). Expression range: focused, collaborative, occasionally smiling — never the exaggerated enthusiasm of stock photography. If someone is looking at a screen, there should be something real on the screen.

## 6. What to Avoid

### Imagery
- **Stock photos of handshakes.** The single most overused image in professional services. It communicates nothing specific and signals that no creative thought was applied. Never use.
- **Gold, mahogany, and "old money" aesthetics.** Dark wood, leather chairs, fountain pens, brass fixtures. This is the visual language of traditional accounting and law firms. It communicates establishment and exclusivity — the opposite of Baseline's positioning. Baseline is accessible, modern, and built for companies that don't have (or want) a corner office.
- **Clip art or illustrated icons in a cartoon style.** Undermines the precision and credibility that Baseline's audience requires. If illustrations are used, they should be geometric, minimal, and constructed from the brand's line weights and color palette.
- **Busy patterns, complex textures, or decorative backgrounds.** The brand's visual strength is whitespace and structure. Any pattern or texture competes with the content and reduces the clarity that is core to the brand promise.
- **Generic "technology" imagery.** Circuit boards, binary code raining down screens, abstract network diagrams, glowing blue orbs. These are visual clichés that signal "we don't know what technology actually looks like." Baseline's audience builds technology. Show them real interfaces, real data, real tools.

### Typography
- **Serif typefaces in primary applications.** Serifs carry traditional/establishment connotations that conflict with the brand's modern positioning. Exception: JetBrains Mono (monospace) uses small serifs for legibility at code sizes, which is functionally appropriate.
- **Handwritten or script fonts.** For any purpose. Including "casual" social media applications.
- **Type set below 13px.** Accessibility and legibility are non-negotiable for a brand built on clarity and precision.

### Color
- **Pure black (#000000) for text.** Use Navy (#1B2B4B) or Slate (#64748B). Pure black against white creates excessive contrast that causes eye strain in long-form reading — and Baseline's audience reads a lot.
- **Using coral as a background color.** It's an emphasis accent only. Large coral surfaces become visually aggressive and undermine the calm authority of the palette.
- **Introducing additional colors.** The palette is complete. If a visualization requires more than six colors, use opacity variants of the existing palette (navy at 80%, 60%, 40%, 20%) rather than introducing new hues.

## 7. Brand World — "The Modern Office"

Baseline's brand world is The Modern Office — not aspirational, not nostalgic, but the actual environment where Baseline's clients work and where the firm's value is delivered. It is a space defined by natural light, clean surfaces, and purposeful technology. Monitors display real dashboards. Whiteboards show real frameworks. The coffee is good but not performative.

This world sits at a specific point on two axes. On the formality axis, it's between "hoodie and flip-flops" and "suit and tie" — think: clean pullover, well-maintained desk, headphones on the desk because they were just being used. On the technology axis, it's between "analog craft" and "sci-fi futurism" — think: a modern laptop running a terminal window next to a notebook with handwritten notes.

The emotional tone of this world is: competent calm. Things are under control. Systems are in place. The work is rigorous but the environment is humane. Nobody is panicking. Nobody is performing. The compliance report will be ready because the process was built correctly from the start.

This is the world Baseline inhabits and the world Baseline helps its clients build.
