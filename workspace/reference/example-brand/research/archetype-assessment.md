# Archetype Assessment: Baseline

**Prepared by:** Archetype Analyst Agent
**Date:** 2026-02-10
**Framework:** Adapted Jungian archetypes, applied to brand positioning and voice strategy

---

## Archetype Summary

| Role       | Archetype       | Derived From | Core Drive                              |
|------------|-----------------|--------------|------------------------------------------|
| Primary    | The Navigator   | The Sage     | Guide others through unfamiliar territory |
| Supporting | The Builder     | The Creator  | Construct durable infrastructure          |

This pairing is uncommon in financial services — most compliance firms default to The Ruler (authority, control, order) or a straight Sage (knowledge for its own sake). Baseline's archetype blend creates a distinctive personality: someone who knows the terrain and builds the path through it. Not a lecturer. Not a gatekeeper. A guide who also carries tools.

---

## Primary Archetype: The Navigator

### Origin and Adaptation

The Navigator is adapted from the Sage archetype but differs in a critical way. The classic Sage seeks and shares knowledge as an end in itself — think research institutions, encyclopedias, consulting firms that produce 200-page reports. The Navigator shares knowledge instrumentally: every insight serves the purpose of getting someone from Point A to Point B. Knowledge isn't the product. Safe arrival is the product.

### Core Motivation
To ensure the client reaches their destination — audit-ready, compliant, financially organized — without unnecessary detours, wasted spend, or false starts.

### Brand Behaviors

**How the Navigator shows up in messaging:**
- Leads with orientation: "Here's where you are. Here's where you need to be. Here's the path." Every service page, sales conversation, and blog post should follow this structure.
- Simplifies without dumbing down. The Navigator respects the intelligence of the traveler while acknowledging they haven't been on this specific road before. "You're smart. You just haven't done this yet."
- Provides stage-appropriate guidance. The Navigator doesn't hand a seed-stage company a map of the entire continent. They show the next 50 miles. This translates to messaging like: "At your stage, here's what matters and here's what doesn't."
- Names risks plainly. The Navigator doesn't use fear tactics, but doesn't minimize hazards either. "If you skip SOC 2 before your enterprise sales push, you will lose deals. That's not a scare tactic — it's what we see every quarter."

**How the Navigator shows up in client relationships:**
- Proactive check-ins before compliance deadlines
- Clear, jargon-light explanations of regulatory requirements
- Willingness to say "you don't need this yet" — even when selling it would be profitable
- Framing every recommendation in terms of the client's specific journey and stage

### Voice Characteristics
- Declarative sentences. Confidence without caveats.
- Spatial and directional metaphors: path, roadmap, milestone, checkpoint, terrain, horizon.
- Calm tone under pressure. When a client is panicking about an upcoming audit, the Navigator doesn't match their energy — they lower it.
- Occasional use of "look" or "here's the thing" as a pivot to unvarnished truth.

## Supporting Archetype: The Builder

### Origin and Adaptation

The Builder is adapted from the Creator archetype but trades artistic expression for structural integrity. The classic Creator wants to make something new and beautiful. The Builder wants to make something that works and lasts. In Baseline's context, this means financial infrastructure, compliance systems, and operational foundations that a startup can grow on top of without rebuilding every 18 months.

### Core Motivation
To construct systems that are durable, scalable, and elegantly functional — not flashy, not over-engineered, just right for the load they need to bear.

### Brand Behaviors

**How the Builder shows up in messaging:**
- Talks about compliance and finance as infrastructure, not chores. "You're not checking a box. You're building the foundation your next 100 hires will stand on."
- Emphasizes craft and precision. The Builder takes pride in clean books, well-structured policies, and audit trails that don't require forensic archaeology to follow.
- Uses construction and engineering metaphors: foundation, framework, scaffold, load-bearing, blueprint.
- Values durability over speed. While the Navigator gets you there efficiently, the Builder ensures what you build along the way doesn't need to be torn down later. "We could get you audit-ready in 30 days with duct tape. Or we could take 45 days and build it right."

**How the Builder shows up in services:**
- Bookkeeping boot camps (constructing capability in clients, not just doing work for them)
- Process documentation that outlives the engagement
- Systems designed for the client's next stage, not just their current one
- Preference for "teach to fish" over "fish for you" — though Baseline does both

### Voice Characteristics
- Concrete, tangible language. Avoids abstraction.
- Process-oriented: steps, phases, layers, components.
- Pride without arrogance. The Builder is quietly satisfied with good work. This shows up as understated confidence rather than chest-beating.
- Occasional dry humor about sloppy work: "We've seen cap tables that would make an accountant cry. We've made some of them cry."

## Archetype Interaction Model

The Navigator and Builder don't compete — they operate in sequence.

**The Navigator** dominates in:
- Top-of-funnel content (blog, social, awareness)
- Sales conversations and discovery calls
- Strategic advisory and scoping
- Crisis moments (audit coming, compliance gap discovered)

**The Builder** dominates in:
- Service delivery and onboarding
- Educational content (boot camps, workshops, how-to guides)
- Post-engagement materials and documentation
- Long-term relationship and account management

**Transition point:** The Navigator says "here's where we need to go." The Builder says "here's how we build it." In practice, a blog post might open with Navigator energy (orientation, context, stage-setting) and close with Builder energy (specific steps, structural recommendations, implementation guidance).

## Shadow Warnings

Every archetype has a shadow — the dysfunctional expression that emerges under stress or misalignment.

### Navigator Shadow: The Know-It-All
- **Trigger:** When expertise curdles into condescension. The Navigator starts to believe the client can't handle complexity and oversimplifies to the point of being patronizing.
- **How it shows up:** Phrases like "trust us, we've got it handled" without explanation. Dismissing client questions. Content that lectures rather than guides.
- **Guardrail:** Always explain the "why" behind recommendations. The Navigator earns trust through transparency, not authority claims. If the team catches itself saying "just trust us," that's the shadow talking.

### Navigator Shadow: The Alarmist
- **Trigger:** When risk-awareness becomes fear-mongering. The Navigator starts overstating consequences to create urgency.
- **How it shows up:** "If you don't act now, you could face catastrophic fines." Artificially compressed timelines. Compliance-as-threat-narrative.
- **Guardrail:** State risks factually and specifically. "SOC 2 non-compliance typically adds 3-6 weeks to enterprise sales cycles" is Navigator. "You could lose everything" is shadow.

### Builder Shadow: The Perfectionist
- **Trigger:** When craftsmanship becomes an excuse for slowness or over-engineering. The Builder insists on a level of polish the client's stage doesn't warrant.
- **How it shows up:** Recommending enterprise-grade compliance frameworks to a 15-person startup. Scope creep justified as "doing it right." Timelines that keep extending because the work is "not ready."
- **Guardrail:** Every recommendation should be stage-appropriate. The Builder's job is to build what's needed now, with an eye toward what's needed next — not to build the final version first.

### Builder Shadow: The Gatecrasher
- **Trigger:** When the desire to build becomes a desire to control. The Builder starts treating the client's systems as its own.
- **How it shows up:** Resistance to handing off documentation. Building processes that require Baseline's continued involvement by design rather than by value. Subtle lock-in.
- **Guardrail:** The best Builder creates things that work without them. Baseline's boot camps are the purest expression of this — building capability in the client, not dependency on Baseline.

## Application to Brand Decisions

When evaluating any content, campaign, or service decision, apply this test:

1. **Does this guide someone toward a destination?** (Navigator check)
2. **Does this build something durable?** (Builder check)
3. **Does this talk down to the audience?** (Navigator shadow check)
4. **Does this over-engineer for the client's stage?** (Builder shadow check)

If the answer to 1 or 2 is no, the work is off-archetype.
If the answer to 3 or 4 is yes, the work is in shadow.
