# Content Audit: Baseline

**Prepared by:** Content Auditor Agent
**Date:** 2026-02-10
**Sources reviewed:** Website (14 pages), LinkedIn (38 posts, Nov 2025 — Jan 2026), Sales deck (42 slides), Email sequences (3 nurture flows)

---

## Executive Summary

Baseline's existing content shows a brand that knows its subject matter deeply but hasn't yet decided how much personality it's allowed to have. The strongest material lives on the blog and in LinkedIn posts written by the founding team. The weakest material is the sales deck, which reads like it was written by a different company — one that's trying to sound bigger than it is. The opportunity is significant: when Baseline sounds like itself, it's one of the most distinctive voices in compliance services.

## What's Working

### 1. The blog hits a genuine teaching tone
Posts like "SOC 2 Type I vs. Type II: What Actually Matters at Your Stage" and "Your First Finance Hire Shouldn't Be a CFO" perform well because they make a clear argument, back it with specifics, and don't hedge. Average read time is 4.2 minutes, which sits in the sweet spot for the audience. The writing trusts the reader to be intelligent without over-explaining foundational concepts.

### 2. LinkedIn posts from the founders carry authentic voice
CEO posts that open with a concrete observation — "Three of our clients got SOC 2 audit-ready in under 60 days this quarter. None of them had a compliance team." — consistently outperform posts that open with broad industry statements. Engagement rate on observation-first posts: 4.7%. On broad-statement posts: 1.2%.

### 3. The homepage headline lands
"Compliance that scales with you." is doing real work. It addresses the core fear (compliance becomes a bottleneck) and the core desire (growth without friction) in five words. User testing notes indicate prospects recall this tagline unprompted after a single site visit.

### 4. Number-forward proof points
When the content leads with specific figures — "94% of our clients pass their first SOC 2 audit" or "Average time-to-compliance: 47 days" — conversion on CTAs increases measurably. The audience responds to quantified outcomes over qualitative promises.

## What's Inconsistent

### 1. The sales deck adopts a corporate mask
Slides 1-10 use phrases like "leveraging synergistic compliance frameworks" and "end-to-end regulatory optimization." This language appears nowhere else in Baseline's content. It reads like the deck was built for a pitch competition judged by people who have never used the product. The disconnect between the deck's voice and the blog's voice would confuse any prospect who encountered both.

### 2. Tone oscillates between peer and authority
Some LinkedIn posts position Baseline as a peer ("We're figuring this out alongside you"), while blog posts and the website position Baseline as an expert guide ("Here's exactly what you need to do"). Both can work, but the inconsistency creates uncertainty about the brand's confidence level. The peer framing undercuts the authority that the audience is paying for.

### 3. Service page descriptions vary wildly in depth
The SOC 2 page has 800 words of genuinely useful content with a clear process breakdown. The PCI compliance page has 120 words that could describe any firm. The bookkeeping boot camp page is the strongest (clear outcomes, pricing transparency, testimonial), while the privacy policy review page reads like a legal disclaimer rather than a service description.

### 4. CTA language is inconsistent
Across 14 pages: "Get Started" (4x), "Book a Call" (3x), "Let's Talk" (2x), "Schedule Your Assessment" (2x), "Learn More" (3x). The most effective CTA in email sequences is "See where you stand" — which frames the action as low-commitment and diagnostic. This language doesn't appear on the website at all.

## Voice Tendencies

**Signature moves that should be amplified:**
- Opening with a specific, slightly surprising data point
- Using "here's the thing" as a pivot to the real insight (appears 7 times across blog posts — it's becoming a signature)
- Short declarative sentences after a complex explanation: "That's it. That's the whole process."
- Occasional dry asides in parentheses: "(Yes, the spreadsheet counts as technical debt.)"

**Patterns that should be retired:**
- Defaulting to "we help companies..." as a sentence opener (appears 23 times across all content)
- Using "navigate" as a verb more than once per piece (compliance firms overuse this — it's becoming invisible)
- Bullet-point lists where a single confident sentence would hit harder
- The word "solutions" (appears 14 times, means nothing every time)

---

## Messaging Gaps

### Not addressed anywhere in current content:
1. **Pricing transparency** — Competitors like Pilot and Bench lead with pricing. Baseline's pricing page says "Let's talk about your needs." For an audience of startup operators who value speed, this is friction.
2. **The "why not DIY" argument** — No content addresses why a 30-person startup shouldn't just use Vanta or Drata's software and skip the services layer. This is the most common objection in sales calls (per team interviews) but is never addressed in content.
3. **Post-engagement support** — What happens after the SOC 2 audit? After the boot camp? Content stops at the point of sale. There's no narrative about ongoing relationship or what Year 2 looks like.
4. **Founder story** — The About page lists credentials but doesn't explain why Baseline exists. The founding story (told informally in podcast appearances) — two ex-Big Four auditors frustrated by watching startups get overcharged for compliance — is compelling and completely absent from owned content.

---

## Recommendations Summary

1. Rewrite the sales deck in the blog's voice. Kill every instance of "leverage," "synergy," and "end-to-end."
2. Commit to the expert-guide positioning. Retire the peer framing — the audience is hiring Baseline because Baseline knows things they don't.
3. Normalize service page depth. Every service page should match the SOC 2 page's structure: what it is, who needs it, how Baseline does it, timeline, and outcomes.
4. Build the "why not software-only" content piece. Address Vanta/Drata directly and honestly — acknowledge what they do well, then articulate where services fill the gap.
5. Standardize CTAs around the diagnostic framing: "See where you stand" as the primary CTA across the site.
6. Publish the founder story. It's the single highest-leverage content gap.
