01

Philosophical Anchor

Before style guides and word lists, there’s a deeper question: what is our job when we write? This section answers that. Everything else in this document is an expression of these three principles.

How We Think

We believe complexity should be navigated, not imposed. Compliance is genuinely complicated — but our job is to make it feel manageable, not to prove how complicated it is. Every piece of communication should leave the reader feeling more capable, not more overwhelmed.

How We Relate to the Reader

We’re the experienced guide, not the gatekeeper. We’ve walked this path hundreds of times. We know where the pitfalls are and how to avoid them. Our role is to share that knowledge — clearly, directly, and without making anyone feel stupid for not knowing it already.

What Never Changes

Regardless of format, audience, or context: we are precise, we are clear, and we respect the reader’s intelligence and time. These three principles are non-negotiable. If a piece of content fails any of them, it doesn’t ship.

02

Voice Summary

Voice is not tone. Tone changes by context — a proposal sounds different from a tweet. Voice is the thing underneath that stays constant. This is Baseline’s voice, distilled.

One-Liner

Writes like a senior accountant who actually enjoys explaining things.

Voice Tags

Precise Clear Confident Pragmatic Direct Occasionally dry Educational

Reader Experience

After reading Baseline content, the reader should feel:

“Okay, I understand this now. These people know what they’re doing, and they’re not going to waste my time.”

Never:

“I feel dumb” or “I still don’t know what to do next.”

Voice in Action

The difference between generic professional writing and Baseline’s voice isn’t dramatic. It’s specific. Here’s what the shift looks like.

03

Writing Style

Voice is who we are. Style is the mechanics of how we put sentences together. This section covers rhythm, structure, and the professional-to-conversational balance that defines Baseline writing.

Sentence and Paragraph Rhythm

  • Average sentence length: 14–18 words. Shorter than academic, longer than casual.
  • Mix of declarative sentences (stating facts) with occasional questions (engaging the reader).
  • Paragraphs: 2–4 sentences. Never a wall of text. Break before the reader needs to.
  • Single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis — but sparingly. Once per piece, max.

Structure Usage

  • Headers liberally — readers scan before they read. Make scanning productive.
  • Numbered lists for sequences — “Step 1, Step 2, Step 3” when order matters.
  • Bullet points for options — when items are parallel, not sequential.
  • Bold for key terms on first use, then normal text after. Don’t bold for emphasis mid-sentence — that’s what sentence structure is for.
  • Tables for comparisons — pricing, timelines, feature sets. Structured data should look structured.

Authenticity Spectrum

80 / 20

80% professional and precise, 20% conversational and human. The professional side establishes credibility. The conversational side establishes trust. Both are necessary.

Professional Register

“SOC 2 Type II certification requires a minimum observation period of six months. We structure our engagements to begin evidence collection on day one, which means most clients achieve certification within eight weeks of engagement start.”

Conversational Register

“Most firms will tell you SOC 2 takes six months. It doesn’t have to. We’ve done this enough times to know exactly what the auditor needs and when — so we start collecting evidence from day one. Eight weeks, start to finish.”

Both are Baseline. The mix depends on context.

Voice Do’s and Don’ts

Rendered as actual text. Not described — demonstrated.

Do: Lead with specifics

“Average time to SOC 2 Type II: 8 weeks. That includes evidence collection, gap remediation, and the audit itself.”

Don’t: Lead with vague claims

“We provide fast, efficient compliance services that get you certified in record time.”

Do: Explain the unfamiliar

“PCI DSS Level 1 requires an annual on-site assessment by a QSA. Translation: a certified auditor verifies your payment systems are secure.”

Don’t: Assume knowledge

“PCI DSS Level 1 QSA assessments must be completed annually per the Council’s mandate.”

Do: Sound like a person

“Your enterprise prospect just asked for SOC 2 documentation. We can have it done in 8 weeks.”

Don’t: Sound like a brochure

“Baseline is a leading provider of SOC 2 compliance solutions for enterprise-ready technology companies.”

04

Signature Moves

Every strong voice has recognizable patterns — techniques that recur often enough to become identifiable but not so often they become tics. These are Baseline’s four signature moves. Each one earns trust in a different way.

Move 01
The Reframe

Taking a compliance concept that sounds intimidating and repositioning it as a business asset. This is the core Baseline move — the one that makes “compliance as growth infrastructure” real in a single sentence.

When to Use Introducing a compliance requirement for the first time
Frequency Once per piece of long-form content
Example “A SOC 2 report isn’t a cost of doing business. It’s the key that unlocks every enterprise deal on your pipeline.”
Move 02
The Plain Translation

Following a technical statement with a plain-English restatement. Respects the reader’s intelligence by keeping the technical term, then respects their time by immediately making it useful.

When to Use After any jargon the audience might not know
Frequency As needed — every unfamiliar term
Example “PCI DSS Level 1 compliance requires an annual on-site assessment by a QSA. Translation: once a year, a certified auditor comes in and verifies your payment systems are secure.”
Move 03
The Honest Qualifier

Proactively addressing what we’re NOT or what we can’t do — building trust through transparency. Most firms only talk about what they can do. We name the edges.

When to Use Sales contexts, service pages, proposals
Frequency Once per major piece
Example “We’re not the cheapest option. If you need a bookkeeper for $500/month, there are good ones out there. We’re for companies that need compliance infrastructure that holds up under scrutiny.”
Move 04
The Concrete Number

Replacing vague claims with specific, defensible numbers. Precision isn’t just a personality trait — it’s a writing technique. Numbers do the work that adjectives can’t.

When to Use Any claim about speed, quality, or results
Frequency At least once in any marketing piece
Example Instead of “We complete SOC 2 audits quickly” → “Average time to SOC 2 Type II: 8 weeks.”
05

Guardrails

Knowing what NOT to do is as important as knowing what to do. These guardrails aren’t arbitrary restrictions — each one protects a specific dimension of trust that Baseline has earned.

Non-Negotiable Rules

Never condescend. The reader may not know what SOC 2 is. That’s fine. They know their business better than we ever will. Respect flows both directions.

Never use fear as a motivator. “You COULD get breached” or “The penalties for non-compliance are severe” — this is true but manipulative. We sell capability, not anxiety.

Never hide behind jargon. If we can’t explain it simply, we don’t understand it well enough. Every technical term gets a plain-English companion.

Never make promises about audit outcomes. We can guarantee our process and our thoroughness. We cannot guarantee a specific audit result — that’s not how auditing works, and implying otherwise is dishonest.

Words and Phrases to Avoid

Avoid Why Say Instead
“Leverage” Corporate jargon “Use” or “build on”
“Synergy” Empty buzzword Say what actually happens
“Best-in-class” Unverifiable claim Be specific about what makes it good
“Turnkey solution” Vague and overused Describe what’s actually included
“Peace of mind” Cliche, sells feeling not substance Describe the actual outcome
“It’s simple” Dismissive — if it were simple, they wouldn’t need us “Here’s how it works”
“Don’t worry” Patronizing Address the concern directly
“Disrupting the industry” Startup cliche, especially ironic for accountants Describe what we do differently

Quality Check

Before publishing anything, ask:
  1. Would I say this to a client sitting across the table? (If not, rewrite it.)
  2. Is there a specific number or example that could replace a vague claim? (If yes, replace it.)
  3. Does the reader know what to do next? (If not, add a clear next step.)
  4. Would a smart person who knows nothing about accounting understand this? (If not, add context.)
06

Scaling by Context

Voice stays constant. Format doesn’t. A tweet and a whitepaper both sound like Baseline, but the professional-to-conversational ratio shifts. Here’s how the voice scales from short-form to long-form.

Short
Short-Form
Social, email subjects, ads
  • Lead with outcomes
  • One idea per post
  • Numbers over adjectives
  • Dry humor welcome
Medium
Medium-Form
Blog, LinkedIn, newsletters
  • Open with the problem
  • Teach something real
  • Headers every 200–300 words
  • End with a takeaway, not a pitch
Long
Long-Form
Guides, whitepapers, case studies
  • Full professional register
  • TOC, sections, executive summary
  • Real data and timelines
  • Strategic conversational moments
S
Short-Form
Social, email subject lines, ads
  • Lead with the outcome, not the service.
  • One idea per post. If you need a comma, you might need two posts.
  • Numbers perform: “8 weeks to SOC 2” > “Fast SOC 2 audits”
  • Dry humor welcome but not forced. If it’s not funny, be useful instead.
Examples
“Your enterprise prospect just asked for SOC 2 documentation. We can have it done in 8 weeks.”
“SOC 2 doesn’t have to take 6 months. Here’s why most firms make it take that long.”
“We’ve never had a client say ‘I wish we’d waited longer to get SOC 2 certified.’”
M
Medium-Form
Blog posts, LinkedIn articles, newsletters · 800–1200 words
  • Open with the problem or situation, not with who we are.
  • Teach something real — the reader should learn even if they never hire us.
  • Structure with headers every 200–300 words.
  • End with a clear takeaway or next step, not a sales pitch.
L
Long-Form
Guides, whitepapers, case studies · 2000–4000 words
  • Full professional register with strategic conversational moments.
  • Comprehensive but structured — table of contents, clear sections, executive summary.
  • Include real data, timelines, and process breakdowns.
  • Case studies: Situation → Approach → Outcome → Timeline → Key Metric
What Stays Constant

Across all formats, always:

Be specific over general
Respect the reader’s time and intelligence
Include at least one concrete number or example
Make the next step clear
Sound like a person, not a firm
